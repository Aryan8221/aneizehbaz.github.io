Interpretable Pneumonia Detection on Chest X-Rays Using ProtoPNet and Deformable ProtoPNet

Elmira Yazdani, Aryan Neizehbaz, Najme Karamzade-Ziarati, Saeed Reza Kheradpisheh
(Submitted to the International Journal of Computer Assisted Radiology and Surgery)
  • This project focuses on developing interpretable AI models for classifying chest X-ray images into three categories: normal, viral pneumonia, and bacterial pneumonia. By utilizing ProtoPNet and its variant, Deformable ProtoPNet, we aimed to build models that not only achieve high accuracy but also provide transparency in their decision-making process.

    ProtoPNet leverages visual prototypes to highlight the key features contributing to each class prediction, making the classification process more interpretable for clinicians. Deformable ProtoPNet further enhances this capability by allowing the model to adapt to variations in X-ray images, capturing subtle patterns specific to each pneumonia type.

    Our models were evaluated based on F1 score, precision, recall, and accuracy, demonstrating their effectiveness as reliable tools for medical diagnostics. This project underscores the potential of interpretable AI to improve clinical decision-making and build trust in AI-driven healthcare applications.

Deep transformer-based dosimetry from pet/ct images

Elmira Yazdani, Aryan Neizehbaz, Saeed Reza Kheradpisheh
(in prep)
  • Radiotherapy treatment planning is crucial for ensuring optimal dose delivery to tumors while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissues. This project presents a comprehensive pipeline for predicting dosimetry using advanced medical image processing and deep learning techniques. Our approach leverages a combination of pre-processing algorithms and the Swin UNETR architecture to enhance dose prediction accuracy.

    Initially, the pipeline involves preprocessing Nifti images, particularly CT and PET scans, through resampling and normalization techniques. This ensures consistent spatial resolution across image modalities, essential for subsequent analysis. The preprocessing module efficiently handles medical imaging datasets, downsampling CT images to match the resolution of PET images, and normalizing them to fixed HU (Hounsfield Unit) ranges.

    The core of our methodology is the implementation of the Swin UNETR model, a state-of-the-art architecture tailored for three-dimensional medical imaging tasks. The model is trained using a curated dataset, leveraging self-supervised learning (SSL) pre-trained weights and gradient checkpointing to optimize both training speed and memory usage. Our training pipeline incorporates a variety of data augmentation techniques, such as random cropping, flipping, intensity scaling, and spatial transformations, to improve model generalization.

    The evaluation of model performance is conducted through extensive metrics, including Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), R-squared (R2), Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR), and Relative Error (RE). The results indicate that our approach achieves high accuracy in predicting dose maps, offering a robust tool for enhancing radiotherapy planning.

    In conclusion, the proposed dosimetry prediction pipeline effectively integrates image preprocessing, deep learning, and robust evaluation metrics to deliver a powerful framework for improving the accuracy of radiotherapy treatments. Our work demonstrates the potential of combining advanced medical imaging techniques with cutting-edge machine learning models to support healthcare professionals in making informed decisions about patient treatment plans.

Employing Enhanced ResNet-Based Architecture for Automatic Segmentation of Tumoral Lesions and Organs on [68 Ga]Ga-PSMA11 PET/CT Images in Prostate Cancer

Elmira Yazdani, Aryan Neizehbaz, Najme Karamzade-Ziarati, Habibeh Vosoughi, Mahdi Sadeghi, Saeed Reza Kheradpisheh, and Parham Geramifar
(Accepted to the 13th Iranian Congress of Medical Physics)

  • Introduction

    Prostate-specific membrane antigen (PSMA) PET/CT has emerged as a powerful tool for quantitative image analysis, particularly in the context of radioligand therapy (RLT) for metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC). However, the manual segmentation of these images remains labor-intensive and prone to errors, limiting its scalability in clinical practice. Recent advancements in deep learning offer a promising solution, enabling faster and more accurate segmentation that reduces dependency on specialized expertise, thereby improving clinical workflow and efficiency.

    Methods

    In this study, we propose an improved ResNet architecture designed to segment lesions and organs at risk (OARs), including salivary glands, liver, spleen, kidneys, bowel, and bladder, in 100 [68 Ga]Ga-PSMA PET/CT images. To handle image variability, normalization and data augmentation techniques were applied. The model, trained over 1000 epochs, used a residual convolutional block to extract deep features from the preprocessed images. For better segmentation accuracy, features from all ResNet layers were combined into a unified output that merged deep semantic and shallow appearance features. The modelâs performance was evaluated using a 5-fold cross-validation, with 80% of the data used for training and 20% for testing. Ground truth segmentation was provided by an expert clinician using 3D Slicer, and the models were implemented using PyTorch and MONAI on an NVIDIA Tesla k80 GPU.

    Results

    The improved ResNet achieved an average Dice similarity coefficient of 0.64 for lesions and 0.82 for OARs, with corresponding precision values of 0.60 and 0.77. Recall values were 0.76 for lesions and 0.91 for OARs, outperforming the benchmark U-Net model. This enhanced ResNet architecture, with its encoder-decoder structure, successfully captured multi-scale contextual features, enabling precise and rapid target identification. The automated segmentation technique presented in this study shows great potential for advancing clinical applications, such as personalized dose prediction in RLT.
